The first AI use case should be boring, frequent, and painful.

The best first AI workflow is rarely the most glamorous one. It is usually a repetitive process with enough structure to evaluate and enough pain that people actually care.

A good first AI use case should be frequent. If the workflow only happens a few times per year, the return on implementation is harder to prove and the team does not build enough usage habits to learn from it.

It should be painful. The pain can be time, quality, delays, inconsistent outputs, manual review, or knowledge trapped in documents. Without pain, adoption depends on novelty, and novelty fades quickly.

It should also be clear enough to evaluate. If nobody can say what good output looks like, the project becomes a demo instead of an operational system. The first workflow should have enough structure to define success criteria, human review points, and failure modes.

This is why boring workflows often make the strongest pilots. They are close to the work, easy to observe, and painful enough that even small improvements compound.
